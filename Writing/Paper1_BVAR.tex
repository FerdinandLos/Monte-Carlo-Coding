\begin{abstract}
\noindent
This paper evaluates specification strategies for estimating structural Impulse Response Functions (IRFs) in Vector Autoregressions (VARs), updating traditional lag-selection evaluations with modern Bayesian methods and set identification. Using Monte Carlo simulations calibrated to the global oil market, we compare information criteria (AIC, BIC, HQC) against Bayesian VARs (BVAR) in recovering structural shocks identified via sign restrictions. Results confirm that AIC outperforms stricter criteria (BIC, HQC) in finite samples by minimizing bias. However, BVAR models generally dominate frequentist lag selection, especially for high-order models, by leveraging prior shrinkage rather than strictly truncating lags. Furthermore, optimizing the shrinkage hyperparameter via Marginal Data Density (MDD) imposes excessive shrinkage, yielding suboptimal structural estimates. This highlights a critical divergence between forecasting and structural inference, concluding that a moderately loose BVAR prior offers a robust approach for accurate IRF recovery.

\vspace{1em}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Structural Vector Autoregression (SVAR), Set Identification, Bayesian VAR (BVAR), Lag Order Selection, Monte Carlo Simulation \\

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Structural Vector Autoregressions (VARs) remain the cornerstone of a large part of empirical macroeconomics, especially for monetary policy, shock transmission, and financial markets. While forecasting may be the main goal for some researchers, many economists also use it to estimate the relationships among factors that determine macroeconomic movements. For this purpose, Impulse Response Functions (IRFs) are derived from the VAR parameters, establishing how a shock in one variable affects the development of another period, both contemporaneously and over horizons of several months or even years. Using this transformation, structural VARs are continually adapted to answer complex questions, such as how speculative trading and real-time news shocks drive global oil market dynamics \citep{kilianRoleInventoriesSpeculative2014, gazzaniOilPriceShocks2024, castelnuovoCommodityPriceShocks2024}, how high-frequency monetary policy surprises transmit through corporate credit spreads \citep{gertlerMonetaryPolicySurprises2015, ferreiraFirmFinancialConditions2023}, and how these policy shifts impact global commodity prices \citep{miranda-pintoMonetaryPolicyTransmission2023}. The specification of the VAR model, as well as the method to decompose it into its structural form, are the two crucial specifications that a researcher has to decide on. Traditionally, VAR models were specified with a lag order determined by economic theory or an information criterion (IC) and then estimated by Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) or Maximum Likelihood (ML). The past 15 years have seen a surge in the popularity of Bayesian methods, in which not a single lag order is specified, but lag orders are weighted and/or shrunk based on a prior and a posterior probability distribution \citep{blakeAppliedBayesianEconometrics}. These methods enable the researcher not only to rely on a single specification but also to incorporate a larger model or set of models while still keeping variance low. The methodology for estimating IRFs has also advanced significantly since \citet{simsMacroeconomicsReality1980} showed that structural IRFs can be retrieved via Cholesky decomposition, i.e., a predetermined causal ordering of the relationships between variables. Newer methods typically require weaker assumptions and adopt a more agnostic approach to inferring economic relationships. The dominant approach nowadays is set identification using sign restrictions informed by economic theory \citep{uhligWhatAreEffects2005,rubio-ramirezStructuralVectorAutoregressions2010}. Unlike in Cholesky decomposition, all variables can simultaneously react to each other's shocks, and sign restrictions can reduce the set of possible IRFs to those that fit the researcher's assumptions. This yields a set of admissible IRFs. Typically, the median target—the specific IRF closest to the point-wise median of the admissible set—is selected as the primary result \citep{frySignRestrictionsStructural2011}.

This study draws inspiration from \citet{ivanovPractitionersGuideLag2005}, the first paper to examine which VAR specification best retrieves the true IRF using Monte Carlo simulations. In 2005, this meant testing criteria for lag selection to assess how well they fit VAR models and retrieve structural IRFs via Cholesky decomposition. We aim to update this analysis to the more recent world of VAR methodology, where IRFs are identified via sign restrictions and estimation relies heavily on Bayesian methods rather than solely on information criteria.. We construct a realistic DGP based on \citet{kilianRoleInventoriesSpeculative2014}'s study of the oil market, using the set identification method. Then we compare different methods across repeated simulations by how well they can retrieve the true IRF, using the median target of their identified set. This study compares the information criteria AIC, BIC, and HQC with BVAR estimates using different overall shrinkage hyperparameters $\lambda_1$. We find that BVARs are a well-performing alternative to OLS-estimated VAR models with IC-selected lag orders, beating the best criterion, AIC, in most scenarios. We could not deduce a clear pattern for which $\lambda_1$-values shall be optimally employed depending on sample size $T$ and true lag order $p_0$. We attribute this to the fact that both higher and lower shrinkage can have the same overall effect: one reduces variance, the other reduces bias. Additionally, we suspect that our simulation still contains too much sampling variance, as computational restrictions did not allow for sufficient iterations. Optimizing $\lambda_1$ via the marginal data density (MDD), a metric geared toward out-of-sample forecasting, generally favors tighter $\lambda_1$-values in order to shrink strongly. Taking the results for different $lambda_1$ specifications into account, this is found to be suboptimal, although still an improvement over AIC in most cases. We hence show that the goals of forecasting and IRF analysis do not necessarily work together in VAR estimation.

\section{Theoretical Background}
\label{sec:theory}
 
\subsection{VAR Framework and IRFs}
\label{sec:VAR_IRF}

The reduced form of a vector autoregression, VAR($p_0$), establishes a relationship between current observations of a set of variables with the past observations of these variables up until a pre-determined lag $p_0$:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:VAR}
    Y_t = v + A_{1}Y_{t-1}+A_2Y_{t-2}+...+A_{p_0}Y_{t-p_0}+e_t
\end{equation}

Here, $Y_t$ is a ($K \times 1$) vector of endogenous variables, $v$ is a vector of intercepts, $A_1, \dots, A_{p_0}$ are ($K \times K$) coefficient matrices, $e_t$ is a white-noise innovation vector with $\mathbb{E}[e_t] = 0$ and covariance matrix $\mathbb{E}[e_t e_t'] = \Sigma$, and $p_0$ denotes the lag order. This is the standard reduced-form representation of a VAR process \citep{lutkepohlNewIntroductionMultiple2005,simsMacroeconomicsReality1980}. However, reduced-form innovations are composed of structural shocks and are therefore uninformative about the effects of any individual shock. To identify the structural shocks of interest, restrictive assumptions must be imposed. The relationship between the two is given by:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:VAR_error}
    e_t = B \epsilon_t
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon_t$ denotes the vector of mutually uncorrelated structural shocks (that are normalized to unit variance, i.e., $\mathbb{E}[\epsilon_t \epsilon_t'] = I$) and $B$ is a matrix satisfying $\Sigma = BB'$. Since there are infinitely many candidate matrices $B$ (for any valid factor $\tilde{B}$ and any orthogonal matrix $D$, the product $\tilde{B} D$ also satisfies $\Sigma = (\tilde{B} D)(\tilde{B} D)'$), restrictive assumptions are required to single out a unique $B$. The literature offers several strategies for this purpose, including short-run (recursive) restrictions \citep{simsMacroeconomicsReality1980,christianoMonetaryPolicyShocks1999}, long-run restrictions \citep{blanchardDynamicEffectsAggregate1989}, and sign restrictions \citep{faustRobustnessIdentifiedVAR1998, uhligWhatAreEffects2005,rubio-ramirezStructuralVectorAutoregressions2010}; see \citet{kilianStructuralVectorAutoregressive2017} for a comprehensive treatment. Following \citet{ivanovPractitionersGuideLag2005}, we do not commit to a single scheme mentioned above but instead adopt, for each data-generating process, the identifying assumptions used in the original study on which it is based.

In general, interpreting the dynamics implied by VAR coefficients is difficult. Therefore, practitioners use Impulse Response Functions (IRF) to summarize the main dynamics that they want to explain. To obtain the coefficient set that constructs the impulse response function, we express the VAR model in terms of an infinite VMA process. Using \eqref{eq:VAR}:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:VMA}
    Y_t = [I-A_1L-A_2L^2-...-A_{p_0}L^{p_0}]^{-1}v + [I-A_1L-A_2L^2-...-A_{p_0}L^{p_0}]^{-1}e_t
\end{equation}
Which is equivalent to: 

\begin{equation} \label{eq:VMA2}
    Y_t = \mu + \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} \Phi_i\,e_{t-i} \quad \text{and} \qquad \Phi_0 = I_K
\end{equation}

where $\mu = [I - A_1 - \cdots - A_{p_0}]^{-1}v$ is the unconditional mean vector and the moving average coefficient matrices are obtained recursively as $\Phi_i = \sum_{j=1}^{i} A_j\Phi_{i-j}$ where $A_j = 0 \quad \text{for} \quad j>p_0$ (see appendix for detailed derivation). Finally, in order to obtain the structural impulse response functions, we plug the expression given in \eqref{eq:VAR_error} into \eqref{eq:VMA2}:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:SVAR}
    Y_t = \mu + \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} \Phi_i\,B\epsilon_{t-i} = \mu + \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} \Theta_i\,\epsilon_{t-i}
\end{equation}

Here, the matrices $\Theta_i$ are the structural impulse response matrices, and they contain the coefficients required to construct the impulse response function. To illustrate, the equation for the first endogenous variable can be written as: 

\begin{equation} \label{eq:SVAR2}
    Y_{1t} = \mu_1 + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \theta_{1k,0} \, \epsilon_{k\,t} + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \theta_{1k,1} \, \epsilon_{k\,t-1} + \sum_{k=1}^{K} \theta_{1k,2} \, \epsilon_{k\,t-2} \, + \, ...
\end{equation} 

where $\theta_{jk,i}$ denotes the response of variable j to structural shock k at horizon i, i.e., the (j,k) element of $\Theta_i$. 

The structural impulse responses in \eqref{eq:SVAR2} depend on the model through two distinct channels. First, the moving average coefficients $\Phi_i$ are determined by the reduced-form autoregressive coefficients $A_1,...,A_{p_0}$, so the impulse responses depend on the lag order $p_0$; in practice, $p_0$ is unknown and must be estimated, which is the subject of the lag-order selection criteria discussed in Section 3. Second, the mapping from reduced-form to structural form impulse responses requires the identification of $B$ introduced in \eqref{eq:VAR_error}. Since there are infinitely many candidate matrices $B$ (for any valid factor $\tilde{B}$ and any orthogonal matrix $D$, the product $\tilde{B} D$ also satisfies $\Sigma = (\tilde{B} D)(\tilde{B} D)'$), restrictive assumptions are required to single out a unique $B$. The literature offers several strategies for this purpose, including short-run (recursive) restrictions, often computed by Cholesky decomposition \citep{simsMacroeconomicsReality1980,christianoMonetaryPolicyShocks1999}, long-run restrictions \citep{blanchardDynamicEffectsAggregate1989}, and sign restrictions \citep{faustRobustnessIdentifiedVAR1998, canovaMonetaryDisturbancesMatter2002, uhligWhatAreEffects2005,rubio-ramirezStructuralVectorAutoregressions2010}; see \citet{kilianStructuralVectorAutoregressive2017} for a comprehensive treatment. We calibrate our data-generating process to the model of \citet{kilianRoleInventoriesSpeculative2014} and accordingly employ a sign-restriction algorithm to identify the impact matrix (though we omit the elasticity bounds for computational feasibility). A key feature of this approach is that sign restrictions deliver only partial identification: rather than a single impact matrix, they identify a \textit{set} of candidate matrices consistent with the a priori restrictions implied by economic theory. This raises several questions about the identification strategy, which are the focus of the next subsection.

\subsection{Sign Restrictions}
\label{sec:sign_restrictions}

Point identification strategies, such as the Cholesky decomposition, impose an assumption restrictive enough to single out a unique impact matrix; in the Cholesky case, this is a recursive ordering that determines the contemporaneous causal structure among the endogenous variables. \citet{uhligWhatAreEffects2005}, however, criticizes such strategies on the grounds that these restrictive assumptions may not reflect the underlying economic reality, but instead may impose the very conclusions the researcher expects to find.

\citet{uhligWhatAreEffects2005} proposes an alternative he terms an "agnostic" identification procedure. The agnostic approach imposes only minimal restrictions---and, crucially, leaves the response of the variable of central interest entirely unrestricted---so that the answer to the research question is determined by the data rather than by the identifying assumptions. The restrictions that are imposed are deliberately weak: they consist only of sign restrictions on a few responses that can be reasonably agreed upon across economists. This minimalism comes at a cost, however. Imposing only sign restrictions does not single out a unique impact matrix; instead, it identifies a set of candidate impact matrices consistent with the assigned signs. This set-identification (or partial identification) strategy is less restrictive, but it comes at the cost of ambiguity, since the object of interest is no longer a single value but a range. Moreover, as emphasized by \citet{uhligWhatAreEffects2005}, sign restrictions introduce a further choice that point-identifying schemes do not: the horizon over which the restrictions are imposed. In addition to specifying the sign of a response on impact, the practitioner must decide how many subsequent periods that sign is required to hold---the longer this horizon, the more candidate models are discarded and the narrower the identified set becomes.

This trade-off is described by \citet{PartialIdentificationProbability2003a} as the Law of Decreasing Credibility: the credibility of inference decreases as the strength of the maintained assumptions increases. Point identification strategies impose stronger assumptions and are therefore more informative—they reduce the identified object to a single point—but they are less credible, since their validity rests entirely on assumptions that may not hold. Partial identification strategies, by contrast, are less informative—they deliver a trimmed range rather than a single element—but more credible, precisely because they rely on weaker and more defensible assumptions. 

Here, we follow \citet{kilianRoleInventoriesSpeculative2014} with slight differences due to computational feasibility.\footnote{\citet{kilianRoleInventoriesSpeculative2014} argue that agnostic sign restrictions alone are not sufficient to narrow the set of admissible models adequately, and they therefore impose additional quantitative restrictions; bounds on the implied oil supply and demand elasticities. Additionally, they set dynamic restrictions for a horizon of 12 months. We omit the elasticity restrictions and reduce the dynamic restrictions to 3 months for reasons of computational feasibility: in our finite samples, the acceptance rate under the elasticity bounds is prohibitively low, so that obtaining a sufficient number of admissible models in each of the many Monte Carlo iterations would require a computationally unachievable number of draws.} First, we fit VAR($p_0$) model to the data of \citet{kilianRoleInventoriesSpeculative2014} and obtain the reduced-form autoregressive coefficients ($\hat A$) and the variance-covariance matrix of the reduced-form errors ($\hat \Sigma$). We then factor this covariance matrix using Cholesky decomposition ($\hat \Sigma = PP'$).\footnote{\citet{kilianRoleInventoriesSpeculative2014} uses eigendecomposition here. However, it doesn't make any difference. Any two factors satisfying $BB' = \tilde B \tilde B' = \hat \Sigma$ differ only by an orthogonal rotation ($B = \tilde B D$). Since our procedure applies the full set of Haar-distributed rotations to the initial factor, the resulting set of admissible models is invariant to the initial decomposition. The only element we need is an initial factor to run the algorithm.} Next, following \citet{rubio-ramirezStructuralVectorAutoregressions2010}, we generate random orthogonal matrices: we draw a matrix of independent standard normal entries, compute its QR decomposition, and normalize the sign of each column so that the diagonal of ($R$) is positive. This procedure yields orthogonal matrices ($Q$) (with $QQ'=I_K$) that are uniformly distributed with respect to the Haar measure, ensuring that the space of admissible rotations is sampled without bias. For each draw, we form a candidate impact matrix ($\tilde B = PQ$) and check whether it satisfies the imposed sign restrictions; candidates that violate the restrictions are discarded. Finally, we retain a select number of admissible impulse response structures and select the median target as our true IRF (population response). For the sake of clarity, we find it appropriate to outline the step-by-step blueprint of this algorithm:

1. Draw a ($K \times K$) matrix $W$, where each element is drawn independently from a standard normal distribution: $W_{ij} \sim N(0,1)$.

2. Compute the QR decomposition $W = QR$, where $Q$ is an orthogonal matrix (the rotation matrix) and $R$ is upper triangular. Normalize the sign of each column of $Q$ so that the diagonal of $R$ is positive, which renders the decomposition unique and ensures that $Q$ is distributed according to the Haar measure.

3. Construct the candidate impact matrix $\tilde B = PQ$, and then check whether $\tilde B$ satisfies the imposed restrictions.

4. Repeat this process by drawing new $W$ matrices (i.e., obtaining new rotation matrices $Q$) until the desired number of admissible models is reached.

5. Among the retained models, select the median target $m^{*}$ as the representative (true) model. 

The selection of a representative model is a much-debated issue in the literature, particularly the comparison between the pointwise median and the median target. Since we must obtain a single population response rather than a set—otherwise the mean squared error (MSE) could not be computed against a unique true IRF—we adopt the approach most appropriate for our design. \citet{frySignRestrictionsStructural2011} criticize the pointwise median for a compelling reason: the pointwise median does not correspond to any single admissible model, as it combines responses drawn from different models with different impact matrices. Consequently, this synthetic profile may appear plausible but need not correspond to any $B$ satisfying $\hat \Sigma = BB'$. This implies that the shocks may not be properly isolated, so the pointwise median cannot be used for variance decomposition. To address this, Fry and Pagan propose the median target approach: 

\begin{equation} \label{eq:Median_Target}
    \begin{aligned} 
        m^* &= \arg\min_m \sum_{j,k,i} \left(\frac{\theta_{jk,i}^{(m)} - \mathrm{med}_{jk,i}}{\sigma_{jk,i}}\right)^2, \\ \text{where}\quad \mathrm{med}_{jk,i} &= \operatorname{median}_m\{\theta_{jk,i}^{(m)}\}, \quad \sigma_{jk,i} = \operatorname{std}_m\{\theta_{jk,i}^{(m)}\}. 
    \end{aligned} 
\end{equation}

Solving this minimization yields the admissible model whose impulse responses are closest to the pointwise median ($m^{*} \in M$) Since $m^{*}$ is an actual admissible model rather than a synthetic construct, it satisfies $\hat \Sigma = BB'$ (which makes sure that shocks are orthogonalized) and can be used for variance decomposition. We adopt this approach due to the coherence concerns described above and take the median target as our representative true IRF whereby its specific impact matrix $\tilde B$ determines the DGP.\footnote{For a visualization of the IRFs selected for the DGP, see Figure~\ref{fig:IRF} in the Appendix.} 

Identification is an essential part of recovering the underlying impulse response function. As noted earlier, however, it is not the only factor that matters: the specification of the reduced-form VAR also plays a role. Recall that $\Phi_i = \sum_{j=1}^{i} A_j\Phi_{i-j}$ where $A_j = 0 \quad \text{for} \quad j>p_0$, so the moving-average coefficients, and hence the impulse responses, depend on the lag order ($p_0$). The practitioner must therefore choose the lag order carefully. We address this in the next section.

\subsection{Information Criteria}
\label{sec:IC}

The true lag order $p_0$ is a priori unknown when estimating a reduced-form VAR; it is a matter of specification. Adding too many lags may lead to overfitting, which inflates the variance of the impulse response estimates by requiring the estimation of additional parameters. Underfitting is also problematic, as it introduces bias. The bias-variance trade-off is therefore central to lag-order selection. Traditionally, information criteria are used to address this issue, and their working principle is essentially the same \citep[chap.~4]{lutkepohlNewIntroductionMultiple2005}:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:IC}
\mathrm{IC}(p) = \ln{|\hat{\Sigma}(p)|} + \underbrace{c_T ,\frac{K^2 p}{N}}{\text{penalty term}}
\end{equation}
Information criteria are formulas that embody the bias-variance trade-off described above. The $\ln|\hat{\Sigma}(p)|$ term is the log-determinant of the reduced-form error covariance matrix, estimated by quasi-maximum likelihood; this term penalizes underfitting, since a model with too few lags leaves $\hat \Sigma(p)$ large and thus raises $\mathrm{IC}(p)$. The Penalty weight $c_T$ depends on the specific criterion, and the penalty term increases with the lag order; the second component therefore penalizes overfitting. The lag order is estimated by:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:lag_selection}
\hat{p} = \arg\min{p} \mathrm{IC}(p), \qquad 1 \leq p \leq \bar{p}
\end{equation}
Following \citet{ivanovPractitionersGuideLag2005}, we set the maximum lag order to $\bar{p}=12$ for our monthly data, corresponding to one year. This choice exceeds the largest true lag order considered $p_0 = 10$, ensuring that all criteria can both identify and overfit relative to the true order, and it makes our results directly comparable to those of \citet{ivanovPractitionersGuideLag2005}.\footnote{We only include AIC, BIC and HQC as information criteria and omit the Likelihood Ratio tests and the Lagrange Multiplier test for lag selection as these have become practically irrelevant in the last 20 years for macroeconomic time series analysis and were also shown to perform poorly in \citet{ivanovPractitionersGuideLag2005}}

Although there are different types of $\mathrm{IC}(p)$, three are mainly used:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:AIC}
\mathrm{AIC}(p) = \ln|\hat{\Sigma}(p)| + \frac{2}{N}, K^2 p
\end{equation}
The Akaike Information Criterion(AIC) \citep{akaikeNewLookStatistical1974} is the least overfitting-averse of these three criteria. It uses $c_T = 2$ and tends to overfit in practice.\footnote{In the literature, the AIC is generally regarded as prone to overfitting. In the VAR setting, however, this tendency is attenuated: adding one more lag requires estimating an entire ($K \times K$) coefficient matrix, so the penalty grows by $\frac {2K^{2}}{N}$ at each step. Because each additional lag is this costly, even the AIC—the most lenient of the three criteria—can underfit in practice.}
\begin{equation} \label{eq:BIC}
\mathrm{BIC}(p) = \ln|\hat{\Sigma}(p)| + \frac{\ln N}{N}, K^2 p
\end{equation}
The Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC), also called the Schwarz Information Criterion \citep{schwarzEstimatingDimensionModel1978}, penalizes overfitting the most. It uses $c_T = \ln {N}$ and is a consistent estimator of the true lag order $\left( \operatorname*{plim}\limits_{N \to \infty} \hat{p}^{\mathrm{BIC}} = p_0 \right)$.
\begin{equation} \label{eq:HQC}
\mathrm{HQC}(p) = \ln|\hat{\Sigma}(p)| + \frac{2\ln\ln N}{N}, K^2 p
\end{equation}
The Hannan-Quinn Information Criterion \citep{hannanDeterminationOrderAutoregression1979} is neither as harsh against overfitting as AIC nor as tolerant as BIC. It uses $c_T = 2 \ln {\ln N}$ as its penalty weight and, like BIC, is a consistent estimator of the true lag order.

The three criteria share the same fit term and differ only in the penalty weight $c_T$, which equals $2$ for the AIC, $2 \ln \ln N$ for the HQC, and $\ln N$ for the BIC. A heavier penalty makes each additional lag more costly and therefore yields a more parsimonious model. Comparing the weights gives:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:IC_comp}
2 ;\leq; 2\ln\ln N ;\leq; \ln N
\end{equation}
where the first inequality holds for $N \ge 16$ and the second for all $N$.\footnote{The weights coincide at $N = e^{e} \approx 15.2$ (for $2 = 2\ln{\ln N}$) and never cross for $\ln N$ versus $2 \ln \ln N$, since $x - 2 \ln x > 0$ for all $x > 0$. The condition $\ln N \ge 2$ (BIC versus AIC) holds for $N \ge 8$. Therefore, $N \ge 16$ is the requirement for (14) to hold.} Consequently, the implied lag-order estimates satisfy:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:IC_comp}
\hat{p}^{\mathrm{BIC}} ;\leq; \hat{p}^{\mathrm{HQC}} ;\leq; \hat{p}^{\mathrm{AIC}}
\end{equation}

so that the BIC is the most parsimonious criterion and the AIC the least, with the HQC in between \citep{ivanovPractitionersGuideLag2005}. Since $N \ge 16$ holds across all sample sizes in our study, this ordering applies throughout. As shown in Table~\ref {tab:ic_lag_orders}, our Monte Carlo Simulation confirms this ordering. The penalization of BIC is evidenced to be so strong that the lag order selected, $\hat{p}^{\mathrm{BIC}}$, always stays close to 2, regardless of the sample size $T$ and the true lag order $p_0$. $\hat{p}^{\mathrm{HQC}}$ lies above $\hat{p}^{\mathrm{BIC}}$ and increases more apparently with $T$; yet it also does not come close to converging to the true lag order. On the other hand, $\hat{p}^{\mathrm{AIC}}$ is consistently much closer to the true lag order and has already nearly reached $p_0$ at $T=600$. Therefore, although AIC is not asymptotically consistent, it is the only IC that can come close to the true lag order for realistic sample sizes.

\citet{lutkepohlComparisonCriteriaEstimating1985} compares information criteria based on their ability to recover the true lag order. \citet{ivanovPractitionersGuideLag2005}, by contrast, object to this approach on the grounds of the bias-variance trade-off discussed above. To see why, it is helpful to consider the decomposition of the mean squared error (MSE) of the estimated median target IRF $\hat \theta$ compared to the true DGP IRF $\theta$:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:MSE}
MSE(\hat \theta) = E[(\hat \theta - \theta)^2] = E[(\hat \theta - E[\hat \theta] + E[\hat \theta] - \theta)^{2}]
\end{equation}

which can be reformulated to:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:MSE2}
MSE(\hat \theta) = E \left[ \left(\hat \theta - E[\hat \theta] \right)^{2} + \underbrace{2\left(\hat \theta - E[\hat \theta] \right)\left(E[\hat\theta]-\theta\right)}_{= 0} + \left(E[\hat\theta]-\theta \right)^{2} \right]
\end{equation}

and finally, after we distribute the expected value operator:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:MSE3}
MSE(\hat \theta) = Var(\hat \theta) + Bias(\hat\theta)^{2}
\end{equation}

we have derived an equation for the bias-variance tradeoff. In small samples, if underfitting produces a reduction in variance large enough to offset the resulting increase in bias, the MSE falls, and if we take the MSE as a proxy for accuracy, this matters more than a criterion's ability to recover the true lag order. The lag order selected by a criterion that underfits may therefore outperform the true lag order; one of the central remarks of \citet{ivanovPractitionersGuideLag2005}.

\subsection{Bayesian VAR (BVAR)}
\label{sec:BVAR}

So far, we have discussed the theoretical elements necessary to replicate \citet{ivanovPractitionersGuideLag2005}. We now extend this framework by introducing Bayesian Vector Autoregression. The frequentist lag-order selection problem and Bayesian shrinkage differ fundamentally. The frequentist approach suffers from the curse of dimensionality: each additional lag adds a full $(K \times K)$ block of coefficients to the system, and because estimation must fit these to the data, every estimated parameter consumes one degree of freedom. Frequentist methods therefore demand large samples—a requirement that becomes acute in our smallest design, where a twelve-lag system requires estimating $K^2p = 192$ autoregressive coefficients against only 240 effective observations per equation.

The Bayesian approach reframes this problem. The estimated value of each parameter is determined jointly by the prior and the data, through the posterior. The posterior mean can be understood as a weighted average of the data and the prior,
\begin{equation} \label{eq:posterior_weight}
\text{posterior} \approx w(\text{data estimate}) + (1 - w)(\text{prior mean})
\end{equation}
where the weight $w$ reflects the tightness of the prior. Because part of each coefficient is supplied by the prior rather than estimated freely from the data, no parameter consumes a full degree of freedom. The prior does not remove parameters; it constrains them, reducing the effective number of parameters the data must support while leaving the nominal dimension unchanged. The curse of dimensionality is thus addressed not by discarding lags, as selection does, but by disciplining their coefficients—at the cost of bias introduced by pulling each coefficient toward the prior mean.

We follow \citet[chapt. 5]{lutkepohlNewIntroductionMultiple2005} in introducing the Bayesian VAR methodology. By Bayes' theorem:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:18}
g(\boldsymbol{\alpha}|\mathbf{y}) = \frac{f(\mathbf{y}|\boldsymbol{\alpha})g(\boldsymbol{\alpha})}{f(\mathbf{y})}
\end{equation}
Here, $\boldsymbol{\alpha}:= \operatorname{vec}(A_1,A_2, \dots, A_p)$; $g(\boldsymbol{\alpha}|\mathbf{y})$ is the posterior distribution—the distribution of the parameters of interest conditional on the data. The term $f(\mathbf{y}|\boldsymbol{\alpha})$ is the probability density of the sample conditional on the parameters, and is algebraically identical to the likelihood function $l(\boldsymbol{\alpha}|\mathbf{y})$; the two differ only in interpretation, depending on whether $\mathbf{y}$ or $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$ is held fixed. The term $g(\boldsymbol{\alpha})$ is the unconditional distribution of the parameters; since the true distribution is unknown, this component represents our prior belief about the parameters. The denominator $f(\mathbf{y})$ is the unconditional density of the sample; for a given sample, it is simply a normalizing constant. It follows from \eqref{eq:18} that the posterior is proportional to the product of the likelihood and the prior:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:19}
g(\boldsymbol{\alpha}|\mathbf{y}) \propto f(\mathbf{y}|\boldsymbol{\alpha})g(\boldsymbol{\alpha}) = l(\boldsymbol{\alpha}|\mathbf{y})g(\boldsymbol{\alpha})
\end{equation}

The practical value of \eqref{eq:19} depends on the forms of the two densities on the right-hand side. We assume that the VAR is Gaussian, so that the reduced-form errors are normally distributed ($e_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma)$). Because the process admits a moving-average representation, each $y_t$ is a linear combination of these innovations and is therefore itself normally distributed; the likelihood is consequently Gaussian in $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$. Therefore, the likelihood function may be written as:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:21}
l(\boldsymbol{\alpha}, \Sigma | \mathbf{y}) = (2\pi)^{-KN/2}|\Sigma|^{-N/2} \exp \left[ -\frac{1}{2} \sum_{t=1}^N e_t’ \Sigma^{-1} e_t \right] , \quad e_t = y_t - v - \sum_{j=1}^p A_j y_{t-j}
\end{equation}

The only remaining element that determines the posterior is the prior $g(\boldsymbol{\alpha})$. Before specifying it, we must articulate our beliefs about the coefficients. First, in the absence of data, we expect each coefficient to fluctuate around a central value, with no reason to favor one direction of deviation over the other; the prior should therefore be symmetric. Second, we require the posterior to be analytically tractable—available in closed form—because the coefficients must be estimated in each of the many Monte Carlo replications, and reliance on numerical methods would be computationally prohibitive. A normal prior—that is, assuming the prior density of $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$ is Gaussian—satisfies both the representation of our belief and the requirement of analytical tractability. Thus, we set our prior as:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:22} 
    g(\boldsymbol{\alpha}) = \left(\frac{1}{2\pi}\right)^{K^2p/2} |V_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}|^{-1/2} \exp \left[ -\frac{1}{2}(\boldsymbol{\alpha} - \boldsymbol{\alpha}^*)'V_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\alpha} - \boldsymbol{\alpha}^*) \right] \quad ,\text{where} \quad \boldsymbol{\alpha} \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\alpha}^{*}, V_\alpha) 
\end{equation}

Since the remaining derivation involves several steps that are not essential to the intuition behind the method, we proceed directly to the result. Product of \eqref{eq:21} and \eqref{eq:22} yields a normally distributed posterior $\boldsymbol{\alpha} \mid \mathbf{y} \sim \mathcal{N}(\bar{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}, \bar{\Sigma}{\boldsymbol{\alpha}})$ where the explicit forms of the moments are given by \citet[chapt. 5]{lutkepohlNewIntroductionMultiple2005}:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:23}
\begin{aligned}
w &:= \begin{bmatrix} V_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}^{-1/2} \boldsymbol{\alpha}^* \\ (I_N \otimes \Sigma^{-1/2})\mathbf{y} \end{bmatrix}, \\
W &:= \begin{bmatrix} V_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}^{-1/2} \\ X' \otimes \Sigma^{-1/2} \end{bmatrix}, \\
\bar{\Sigma}_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}} &:= (W'W)^{-1} = \left[ V_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}^{-1} + (XX' \otimes \Sigma^{-1}) \right]^{-1}, \\
\bar{\boldsymbol{\alpha}} &:= (W'W)^{-1} W'w = \left[ V_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}^{-1} + (XX' \otimes \Sigma^{-1}) \right]^{-1} \left[ V_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}^{-1} \boldsymbol{\alpha}^* + (X \otimes \Sigma^{-1})\mathbf{y} \right]
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Here, $\bar{\boldsymbol{\alpha}}$ is the posterior mean, which we take as our Bayesian VAR coefficient estimate. A remaining difficulty is that computing the posterior mean requires an estimate of the covariance matrix $\Sigma$, and a full least-squares estimate of $\Sigma$ would itself be subject to the curse of dimensionality. Two approaches address this, which we discuss below.

\subsection*{Minnesota Prior}

The Minnesota prior is a special case of the normal prior ($\boldsymbol{\alpha} \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\alpha}^{}, V_\alpha)$) \citep{doanForecastingConditionalProjection1984, littermanForecastingBayesianVector1986}. We discussed above that the coefficients should fluctuate around the central value. Minnesota prior specifies the central value ($\boldsymbol{\alpha}^{}$) and the amplitude of the fluctuation ($V_\alpha$) based on the a priori belief that the macro variables tend to follow a random walk. We use first-differenced variables in our study. Therefore, we follow the Minnesota Prior and form a belief that suggests that our variables follow a white noise procedure (if a series follows a random walk in levels, its first difference is white noise). This implies that we set $\boldsymbol{\alpha}^{*} = 0$ and $V_\alpha \neq 0$. However, the exact specification of $V_\alpha$ is itself a substantial topic in the Bayesian VAR literature.

The Minnesota prior encodes its beliefs about the coefficients through the prior variance ($V_\alpha$), which is governed by a small set of hyperparameters (See \citet{dieppeBEARToolbox2016} for detailed hyperparameter choices and specifications regarding other priors). Each controls a distinct aspect of how strongly the coefficients are shrunk toward the prior mean. Following \citet{littermanForecastingBayesianVector1986}, the prior variance of a coefficient depends on whether it relates a variable to its own lags or to the lags of other variables. We now present the specification that the Minnesota prior imposes on ($V_\alpha$):
\begin{equation} \label{eq:24}
\sigma_{a_{ii}}^2 = \left( \frac{\lambda_1}{l^{\lambda_3}} \right)^2
\end{equation}
Here, $\sigma_{a_{ii}}^2$ is the prior variance for a coefficient relating variable $i$ to lag $l$ (so, for instance, the coefficient of one lagged oil price in the oil price model). $\lambda_1$ is the overall tightness hyperparameter—the central choice left to the researcher—which sets the overall strength of the shrinkage. High $\lambda_1$ increases the variance (uncertainty around the prior belief), therefore lets the data dominate, and small $\lambda_1$ shrinks the variance and prioritizes the prior belief. Two extreme cases are $\lambda_1 = 0$ and $\lambda_1 = \infty$, where the former returns the prior mean (our belief), and the latter returns the OLS estimate (only data matters). $l$ stands for the lag difference (so if it is the prior variance of the coefficient of one lagged oil price in the oil model, $l = 1$) and $\lambda_3$ is the lag decay. Lag decay governs how quickly the prior variance declines with the lag length $l$, through the factor $l^{\lambda_3}$. It encodes the belief that coefficients on more distant lags are more likely to be near zero; a larger $\lambda_3$ shrinks distant lags more aggressively.

Minnesota prior also specifies the prior variance for a coefficient relating a cross-variable $j$ to lag $l$:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:25}
\sigma_{a_{ij}}^2 = \left( \frac{\sigma_i}{\sigma_j} \right)^2 \left( \frac{\lambda_1 \lambda_2}{l^{\lambda_3}} \right)^2
\end{equation}
Parameters described above remain the same. There is an additional $\lambda_2$ hyperparameter which accounts for cross-variable tightness. This factor, with $0 < \lambda_2 < 1$, applies only to coefficients on the lags of other variables. It encodes the belief that a variable's own past is more informative about its current value than the past of other variables, and therefore shrinks cross-variable coefficients more strongly than its own lags. Setting $\lambda_2 = 1$ removes this distinction, treating own and cross-variable lags symmetrically. $\left( \frac{\sigma_i}{\sigma_j} \right)^2$ component stands for scale adjustment. Because the variables differ in their units and volatility, the ratio of residual standard deviations from univariate autoregressions rescales the cross-variable variances, so that the prior is not distorted by the arbitrary scale of each variable (specifically, $\sigma_i$ and $\sigma_j$ are the residual standard deviations of the univariate AR model of each endogenous variable within the system).

We also have deterministic variables (i.e., seasonal dummies and constants) in our equations within the VAR system. Minnesota prior uses the following specification for the prior variances of the coefficients related to deterministic variables:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:26}
\sigma_{c_i}^2 = \sigma_i^2(\lambda_1 \lambda_4)^2
\end{equation}
In specification \eqref{eq:25}, the hyperparameter $\lambda_4$ sets the prior tightness on the exogenous and deterministic terms—in our specification, the intercept and the eleven monthly seasonal dummies. These are typically left loosely restricted, reflecting the absence of strong prior beliefs about their values.

The package that we use, \texttt{Alexandria}, sets $\lambda_3 = 1$ and $\lambda_4 = 100$, typical values that are used in literature (see \citet{dieppeBEARToolbox2016,alexandria}). Minnesota prior then still leaves two parameters, $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$, to be chosen by the practitioner. We try different $\lambda_1$ values: we try fixed parameter values $\lambda_1 \in \{0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 0.8\}$ as well as selecting the $\ lambda_1$ value from this set that maximizes the Out-of-Sample performance by MDD calculation. The prior that we use (Normal-Wishart Prior, which is discussed below) forces $\lambda_2 = 1$, implying no additional tightness for cross variables.

To sum up, the prior variance structure specified by the Minnesota prior looks like:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:27}
V_{\alpha}^{\text{Minn}} = \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix}
\lambda_1^2 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & \cdots \\
0 & \left( \frac{\sigma_1}{\sigma_2} \right)^2 (\lambda_1 \lambda_2)^2 & \cdots & 0 & \cdots \\
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\
0 & 0 & \cdots & \left( \frac{\lambda_1}{l^{\lambda_3}} \right)^2 & \cdots \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots
\end{pmatrix}}_{((K^2P+12K) \times (K^2P+12K)) = ((16P+48) \times (16P+48)) }
\end{equation}
Here, \eqref{eq:27} demonstrates prior beliefs regarding variance information of coefficients within the system. We have $ K^2p$ lagged variables and 11 seasonal dummies + 1 constant (12 deterministic variables per model). Therefore, we estimate $ K^2p + 12K$ coefficients in total.

Under the Minnesota prior with $\boldsymbol{\alpha}^{*} = 0$, the posterior mean in \eqref{eq:23} simplifies to:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:28}
\bar{\boldsymbol{\alpha}} = \left[(V_\alpha^{\text{Minn}})^{-1} + (X'X \otimes \Sigma^{-1})\right]^{-1}(X \otimes \Sigma^{-1})\mathbf{y}
\end{equation}
where $(V_\alpha^{\text{Minn}})$ is the Minnesota prior covariance defined in \eqref{eq:27}. This estimator resembles the least-squares estimator augmented by the prior precision term $(V_\alpha^{\text{Minn}})^{-1}$, which shrinks the coefficients toward zero; the strength of this shrinkage is governed by the tightness hyperparameter $\lambda_1$.

The covariance $\Sigma$ that appears in the posterior mean formula is not taken from the full VAR least-squares estimate - which would itself be subject to the curse of dimensionality - but is obtained from the univariate autoregressions used to set the prior scale \citep{karlssonForecastingBayesianVector2012}. In our Normal-Wishart implementation, $\Sigma$ is instead treated as unknown and estimated jointly with the coefficients, as described below.

\subsection*{Normal-Wishart Prior}

The Minnesota prior developed above treats the residual covariance matrix $\Sigma$ as fixed, replacing it with an estimate obtained from univariate autoregressions. This is a simplification: in reality, $\Sigma$ is unknown and is estimated alongside the coefficients. The Normal-Wishart prior relaxes this assumption by treating $\Sigma$ as a random parameter and assigning it a prior, so that the uncertainty about $\Sigma$ is propagated into the posterior rather than ignored. The prior is specified jointly over the coefficients $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$ and the covariance $\Sigma$ as \citet{dieppeBEARToolbox2016}:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:29}
\Sigma \sim \mathcal{IW}(S_0, \alpha_0), \quad \boldsymbol{\alpha} \mid \Sigma \sim \mathcal{N}(\alpha^*, \Sigma \otimes \Phi_0)
\end{equation}
The Inverse-Wishart distribution is used to model the residual covariance matrix because a normal distribution cannot guarantee the properties a covariance matrix requires: symmetry and positive definiteness.

In prior \eqref{eq:29}, $\Sigma$ is given an Inverse-Wishart prior with scale matrix $S_0$ and degrees of freedom $\alpha_0$. The scale matrix sets the prior mean of the covariance through $E[\Sigma] = S_0/(\alpha_0 - K - 1)$, while $\alpha_0$ controls the strength of the prior; following \citet{dieppeBEARToolbox2016} we set $\alpha_0 = K + 2$ (here 6), the smallest value yielding a well-defined prior mean. The coefficients ($\boldsymbol{\alpha}$), conditional on $\Sigma$, retain a normal prior with mean $\alpha^{*}$ (set to zero, as before) and covariance ($\Sigma \otimes \Phi_0$). The matrix $\Phi_0 = \mathrm{diag} \left( \frac{1}{\sigma_1^2} \lambda_1^2, \dots, \frac{1}{\sigma_j^2} \left( \frac{\lambda_1}{l^{\lambda_3}} \right)^2, \dots, (\lambda_1 \lambda_4)^2 \right)$ is the analogue of $V_\alpha^{\text{Minn}}$, but with a crucial difference: it specifies the prior variance for the coefficients of a single equation, which is then scaled across equations by the Kronecker product with $\Sigma$ \citep{dieppeBEARToolbox2016}.

This conjugate structure, an Inverse-Wishart prior on $\Sigma$ combined with a conditionally normal prior on $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$, keeps the posterior in the same Normal-Inverse-Wishart family, so that the posterior mean remains available in closed form. The cost of this tractability is a restriction on the prior variance structure, which we now make explicit.

Coefficients' conditioned variance covariance matrix ($\Sigma \otimes \Phi_0$) has to be modeled in that way in order to assure the existence of the closed form solution (conjugate posterior). Since $\Phi_0$ is the same diagonal matrix for every model, this prior forces the practitioner to set $\lambda_2$ hyperparameter equal to one, so that imposing looser or tighter shrinkage on cross-variable coefficients is not possible under the Normal-Wishart prior—the cost of its analytical convenience.\footnotemark[6]

Overall, we use a method in our study that takes the uncertainty of the variance-covariance matrix of the reduced form innovations by enlarging the prior’s definition. This approach is more realistic, but it comes at a cost: to preserve conjugacy, the prior covariance of the coefficients must take a specific Kronecker structure. Because $\Phi_0$ is shared across all equations, a given position in $\Phi_0$ corresponds to an own-lag coefficient in one equation but a cross-variable coefficient in another; the single shared $\Phi_0$ therefore cannot assign them different shrinkage, which forces $\lambda_2 = 1$. That is why \citet{dieppeBEARToolbox2016} suggests that the $\lambda_1$ hyperparameter should be set more tightly when the Normal-Wishart prior is used.

Having established the theoretical framework and estimation methodology, we now turn to the simulation design to evaluate the empirical performance of the competing approaches.

\section{Simulation Design}
\label{sec:simulation}

This Monte Carlo simulation study follows \citet{ivanovPractitionersGuideLag2005} design with several alterations in order to make our extensions feasible and to fit the needs of our analysis. First of all, we only use one monthly DGP based on the empirical VAR study of \citet{kilianRoleInventoriesSpeculative2014}. Therefore, we do not get estimations for quarterly VAR series or VEC series, and additionally, we do not have additional robustness by including several different series with different macroeconomic indicators and movements. The reason for this limitation is our tight time constraint. We use the dataset from \citet{kilianRoleInventoriesSpeculative2014} containing the following 4 variables: percent change in global oil production, real activity index from \citet{kilianNotAllOil2009}, the log real price of oil, and changes in OECD crude oil inventories. Just as in the original study, we first estimate a VAR with a set lag order of these 4 variables and then use the sign restrictions approach explained earlier to identify structural IRFs and select the median target.

The DGP is formed by structural VAR model using the structural impact matrix $\tilde{B}$ that corresponds to the median target IRF as the parameter for the structural shocks $e_t$, which are drawn from an i.i.d. standard normal distribution. The formula for the DGP is represented by a structural VAR model using the estimated reduced form parameters $A$ (for the lag coefficients $y_{t-p}$) and $V$ (for the monthly dummy parameters $d_t$) and the structural impact matrix $\tilde{B}$ as follows:

\begin{equation}
    y_t = V d_t + A y_{t-p} + \tilde{B} e_t
\end{equation}

We estimate these parameters using the original data for two different lag orders, $p_0 \in \{4,10\}$ and create DGPs with different sample sizes, $T \in \{240, 360, 480, 600\}$, where $T=N-\bar{p}$. $\bar{p}$ is the set of lag orders that are considered for both the IC and the BVAR estimations; it is uniformly set to $\bar{p}=12$ to ensure that the true lag order $p_0$ is part of the set. We restrict the lag order selection $p_0$ to only include one high and one low lag order and drop $T=300$ in comparison to the setup of \citet{ivanovPractitionersGuideLag2005} in order to save computational power for the sign restriction decomposition. For sampling, we use random draws of length $p_0$ from the original dataset as initial values, then apply the parametric bootstrap using the SVAR described above and generate sequences of lengths $T$.

On each of these samples, VARs fitted by lag orders selected by both the three ICs as well as different specifications of BVAR are estimated, a fixed number of admissible IRFs found, and the median target IRF determined. We estimate all BVARs using $p=\bar{p}=12$ as the lag order, thereby solely relying on shrinkage for reducing the variance and bias of the estimation. 

Finally, we estimate the key performance parameter, the relative MSE, by calculating the geometric mean over the ratio of the squared errors of the median target IRFs $\hat \theta_j$ computed by method j in relation to the squared errors of the median target IRF by a model fit with the true lag order $\hat \theta_{p_0i}$ over all Monte Carlo iterations from $1, ..., R$:

\begin{equation}
    MSE^{relative}_j = \exp\left( \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{R} \ln(\frac{SE_{ji}}{SE_{p_0i}}) \right)
\end{equation}

The geometric mean is preferred over the arithmetic mean as the ratios are bounded on the left side at 0, but unbounded on the right side. Outliers on the right side are hence overweighed in the arithmetic mean. Normalization by the OLS VAR estimation with $p_0$ ensures that the performance in each iteration is weighed by how well a correctly identified model performs.

For the computation of the DGP parameters, we set the number of selected admissible IRFs to 1 million in order to get a highly accurate representation of the real world movements. However, during the Monte Carlo simulation we have to reduce that number to 1,000, which is seen as the minimum requirement for accurate set identification, albeit still with a larger variance. We set the number of Monte Carlo iterations to $R=500$. We understand the concern that this may be too little for eliminating all Monte Carlo biases. Yet we have to rely on such a small number as it is the absolute maximum that our hardware allows for, even though we have optimized the code by employing parallel processing and delegation to C-based processes using the \texttt{numba} and \texttt{joblib} packages in \texttt{python}. All in all, this simulation still took 18 hours to complete with 6.5 million admissible IRFs drawn.

For a low lag order, OLS can still estimate all parameters with relatively small variance. However, for a high lag order such as $p_0=10$, estimating all parameters produces unstable estimates. Here, the shrinkage of further lag orders introduced in BVAR is a clear advantage. It enables the model to capture the signal from higher lag orders without adding too much noise. Additionally, as we follow the common approach to fit a general high lag order of $\bar p =12$ to both cases of $p_0$, the BVAR for $p_0=4$ includes more lags that are beyond the true underlying limit. Although they are shrunk towards 0 based on the prior and the computed posterior probabilities, they still introduce a bias.

Over both $p_0$s, we find that the BVAR estimation using $\lambda_1 \in \{0.2,0.4,0.6\}$ has more instances of outperforming than underperforming AIC, while $\lambda_1=0.8$ loses the comparison to the AIC benchmark in the majority of cases. 

Overall, the medium-loose prior $\lambda_1=0.6$ is the best hyperparameter in four cases. Both $\lambda_1=0.2$ and $\lambda_1=0.4$ are dominant in two scenarios. The very loose prior of $\lambda_1=0.8$ is shown to be optimal in no case. There is no absolutely clear pattern in terms of sample size or true lag order for why certain $\lambda_1$-values perform better in certain scenarios than in others, neither when you consider all scenarios nor when you separate the analysis by $p_0$. 

This may be explained by two reasons. First, the inherent bias-variance trade-off in shrinkage implies that both strong shrinkage, through reduction of variance, as well as weak shrinkage, through reduction of bias, can make the estimation more precise. This may explain results in certain scenarios, such as the combination of $p_0$ and $T=10$, where the relative MSE of the tightest hyperparameter, $\lambda_1=0.2$, is very close to that of the loosest, $\lambda_1=0.8$. $\lambda_1=0.8$ ensures that higher lag orders are still more relevant for the estimation, making it fit better to the true model of lag order $p_0=10$. Meanwhile, $\lambda_1=0.2$ can reduce the variance of low lag orders, making a few coefficients more accurate while neglecting others. Second, the hardware-forced limitations on the number of iterations and the number of required admissible IRFs per model may make the results too unstable. It can therefore not be refuted that some of the variation among our results is driven by random sampling variations.

Interestingly, the MDD-optimized $\lambda_1$ also never takes first place among the BVAR models. Figure~\ref{fig:mdd_surface} reveals the reason. For low sample sizes $T$ as well as the lower true $p_0=4$, MDD purely selects the lowest option $\lambda_1=0.2$, which has been shown not to perform the best in these cases. Only for $p_0=10$ does it select a higher $\lambda_1=0.4$ in some iterations. However, both $\lambda_1=0.2$ and $\lambda_1=0.4$ do not dominate for these scenarios. Again, the weak performance of these specific $\lambda$-values might be partially attributed to sampling error. Nevertheless, it shows that MDD might not necessarily select the optimal hyperparameter for the purpose of the inference of structural relationships by VAR, as its optimization on out-of-sample performance generally leads to a preference towards stronger shrinkage, which might not be optimal for constructing IRFs, as it eliminates the signal from higher lag orders.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This study evaluates the specification of Structural Vector Autoregressions (VARs) for the purpose of impulse response analysis, updating the foundational framework of \citet{ivanovPractitionersGuideLag2005} to reflect contemporary empirical practices. By constructing a realistic data-generating process based on the global oil market model of \citet{kilianRoleInventoriesSpeculative2014} and employing set identification via sign restrictions, we compare the efficacy of traditional information criteria against Bayesian VAR (BVAR) shrinkage in recovering the true median target Impulse Response Function (IRF).

Our Monte Carlo simulation results confirm that among traditional information criteria, the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) consistently outperforms the Bayesian (BIC) and Hannan-Quinn (HQC) criteria in finite samples. The stricter penalization of the BIC and HQC leads to severe underfitting, introducing a bias that outweighs the benefits of reduced variance. In contrast, the AIC's milder penalty allows for a closer approximation of the true lag order $p_0$, making it the most robust frequentist choice for structural inference. 

More importantly, our findings demonstrate that BVAR models mostly dominate ordinary least squares estimations reliant on IC-selected lag orders. This advantage becomes particularly pronounced when the true underlying lag order is large (e.g., $p_0 = 10$). Rather than strictly truncating the lag length and risking omitted variable bias, the BVAR approach retains higher-order lags while applying prior shrinkage to discipline the parameter space. This continuous bias-variance trade-off consistently yields more accurate structural IRFs than the binary selection mechanism of information criteria.

However, the optimal degree of shrinkage remains ambiguous. We find no strictly dominant hyperparameter $\lambda_1$ across all sample sizes and true lag orders, as both tighter and looser priors can successfully minimize the mean squared error through different channels—variance reduction and bias reduction, respectively. While a medium-loose prior ($\lambda_1 = 0.6$) most frequently achieved the lowest relative MSE in our simulations, the performance differentials were sometimes marginal. We suspect that residual sampling variance, driven by computational constraints that limited our Monte Carlo study to 500 iterations, contributes to this ambiguity. Future research leveraging greater computational resources could further clarify these parameter boundaries by enabling a higher volume of iterations and admissible IRF draws.

Finally, we highlight a critical divergence between forecasting and structural analysis objectives. Optimizing the shrinkage parameter $\lambda_1$ via Marginal Data Density (MDD)—a metric geared toward out-of-sample forecasting—tends to favor excessively tight priors. While this heavy shrinkage successfully minimizes out-of-sample prediction error, it overly dampens the signal from higher-order lags, yielding suboptimal estimations of the structural IRFs. Ultimately, we show that specification strategies designed to maximize forecasting accuracy do not necessarily align with the goal of accurately identifying macroeconomic structural relationships. For practitioners prioritizing structural inference over pure forecasting, BVAR estimation leveraging a moderately loose prior provides an effective methodological approach.

\section{Derivation of the Relationship Between Structural Form Innovation Terms and Reduced Form Errors}

For clarity, several intermediate steps were omitted in the main text. We collect them here. To derive the relationship in equation \eqref{eq:VAR_error}, we begin from the structural form:
\begin{equation}
Y_t = B_0Y_t + B_1Y_{t-1} + B_2Y_{t-2} + \cdots + B_{p_0}Y_{t-p_0} + \varepsilon_t  
\end{equation}

Moving the contemporaneous term $B_0Y_t$ to the left-hand side gives:
\begin{equation}
\underbrace{(I-B_0)}_{B^*}Y_t = B_1Y_{t-1} + B_2Y_{t-2} + \cdots + B_{p_0}Y_{t-p_0} + \varepsilon_t  
\end{equation}

Premultiplying by the inverse of $B^*$ yields the reduced form:
\begin{equation}
Y_t = \underbrace{(B^*)^{-1} B_1}_{A_1} Y_{t-1} + \underbrace{(B^*)^{-1} B_2}_{A_2} Y_{t-2} + \cdots + \underbrace{(B^*)^{-1} B_{p_0}}_{A_{p_0}} Y_{t-p_0} + \underbrace{(B^*)^{-1} \varepsilon_t}_{e_t}  
\end{equation}
which is the explicit reduced-form representation. The relationship defined in \eqref{eq:VAR_error} is now apparent: writing $(B^*)^{-1} = B$, we have:
\begin{equation}
e_t = B \varepsilon_t  
\end{equation}

Here, $B_0$ and $B$ denote distinct objects: $B_0$ is the structural contemporaneous coefficient matrix, which governs the simultaneous interactions among the endogenous variables in the structural form, whereas $B = (I-B_0)^{-1}$ is the impact matrix that maps the unit-variance structural shocks $\varepsilon_t$ into the reduced-form innovations $e_t$. The former is a primitive of the structural model; the latter is the object recovered through the identification procedure in the main text.

\section{Derivation of the Recursive Coefficients and the Mean}

We now derive the recursion in (4). It is convenient to define the autoregressive lag polynomial: 
\begin{equation}
A(L) = I - A_1L - A_2L^2 - \cdots - A_{p_0}L^{p_0}  
\end{equation}

Its inverse admits a power-series representation in the lag operator:
\begin{equation}
A(L)^{-1} = \Phi(L) = \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} \Phi_i L^{i} = \Phi_0 + \Phi_1L + \Phi_2L^2 + \cdots  
\end{equation}

Which leads to: 
\begin{equation}
A(L)A(L)^{-1} = A(L)\Phi(L) = (I - A_1L - A_2L^2 - \cdots)(\Phi_0 + \Phi_1L + \Phi_2L^2 + \cdots) = I  
\end{equation}

Matching coefficients on equal powers of $L$ gives: 
\begin{align*}
I\Phi_0 &= I_K \implies \Phi_0 = I_K \\
\Phi_1 - A_1\Phi_0 &= 0 \implies \Phi_1 = A_1\Phi_0 \\
\Phi_2 - A_1\Phi_1 - A_2\Phi_0 &= 0 \implies \Phi_2 = A_1\Phi_1 + A_2\Phi_0
\end{align*}
and, in general, the recursion: 
\begin{equation}
\Phi_i = \sum_{j=1}^{i} A_j\Phi_{i-j}  
\end{equation}

The unconditional mean is $\mu = (I-A_1-A_2-\cdots-A_{p_0})^{-1}v$ provided the process is weakly stationary. To derive it, take expectations of (1) under weak stationarity, so that $\mathbb{E}[Y_t] = \mu$ for all $t$:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[Y_t] = v + A_1\mathbb{E}[Y_{t-1}] + A_2\mathbb{E}[Y_{t-2}] + \cdots + A_{p_0}\mathbb{E}[Y_{t-p_0}] \iff [I-A_1-A_2-\cdots-A_{p_0}]\mu = v  
\end{equation}

which gives: 
\begin{equation}
\mu = [I-A_1-A_2-\cdots-A_{p_0}]^{-1}v  
\end{equation}
